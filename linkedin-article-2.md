# The OSPO as Institutional Infrastructure: Why Open Source Governance Is Now a Regulatory Imperative

*Published by IAAUTECO Inc. | Open Source Systems & Sovereign AI*

---

There is a version of the Open Source Program Office conversation that remains trapped in the language of engineering culture: inner source champions, upstream contribution metrics, developer advocacy. It is an important conversation. It is not a sufficient one.

The more consequential conversation — the one that belongs in the offices of Chief Information Officers, General Counsel, Chief Information Security Officers, and institutional boards — concerns a different set of questions. Questions about legal liability. Questions about supply chain security. Questions about regulatory compliance. Questions about sovereignty.

The OSPO, properly understood, is not a technical programme. It is **institutional infrastructure**.

## The Regulatory Landscape Has Changed

For European institutions, the governance obligations of open source are no longer abstract or voluntary. They are being written into law.

The **NIS2 Directive**, which entered force in October 2024, imposes supply chain security requirements on essential and important entities that cannot be met without systematic visibility into software dependencies — precisely the kind of visibility that an OSPO is designed to provide. The organisation that cannot produce an accurate Software Bill of Materials for its critical systems is not merely operationally immature. It is potentially non-compliant.

The **EU Cyber Resilience Act**, currently entering its implementation phase, extends security obligations to products with digital elements — including software. It requires manufacturers and publishers to manage vulnerabilities in open source components systematically, to maintain SBOM documentation, and to report actively exploited vulnerabilities. These are OSPO functions.

The **Interoperable Europe Act** mandates openness and interoperability as foundational principles of public digital infrastructure. Publishing public software as open source — with appropriate licence governance — is increasingly an institutional obligation, not a choice.

These are not peripheral regulatory curiosities. They are the legislative expression of a political consensus that open source software is strategic infrastructure, and that its governance must meet the standards applied to other forms of critical institutional infrastructure.

## The Cost of the Governance Gap

The organisations that lack formal open source governance structures are not merely missing an opportunity. They are accumulating risk.

**Legal risk** accrues with every untracked dependency. A copyleft licence incorporated into a distributed product without OSPO review can expose the organisation to intellectual property claims and reputational damage of significant magnitude. The frequency of such incidents is rising as licence enforcement activity increases across the open source ecosystem.

**Security risk** compounds with every unmonitored component. The Log4Shell vulnerability in 2021 affected an estimated 3 billion devices. The XZ Utils supply chain attack in 2024 came within hours of compromising a significant proportion of Linux-based SSH infrastructure globally. These were open source supply chain failures — the kind that SBOM-based monitoring and OSPO-governed dependency management are designed to detect.

**Strategic risk** materialises in dependency. The organisation that consumes open source without contributing to it has no governance voice in the projects that govern its operational continuity. Upstream decisions — deprecations, licence changes, security policies — are made by communities in which the dependent organisation has no standing.

**Sovereign risk** is the aggregate of all of the above, compounded by governance structures that defer to non-European platforms, non-European standards, and non-European legal frameworks. For European public institutions, this is not merely a strategic inconvenience — it is a political failure with accountability implications.

## What an OSPO Actually Requires

An effective OSPO requires four things that are frequently absent from informal open source governance arrangements:

**Formal institutional mandate.** An OSPO without authority to enforce policy, require disclosure, and allocate resources is an advocacy function. Advocacy does not produce compliance.

**Policy architecture across the full lifecycle.** Open source governance is not a single policy — it is a family of policies: consumption, contribution, publication, security, inner source, AI/model governance. Each requires a dedicated instrument, maintained and reviewed on a defined cycle.

**Standards-aligned processes.** OpenChain ISO/IEC 5230, SPDX, REUSE, CHAOSS — the international standards community has developed authoritative, auditable frameworks for every dimension of open source governance. An OSPO that builds on these standards is auditable, comparable, and interoperable with partner organisations' governance structures.

**Metrics and public accountability.** A governance function without metrics is a governance function without accountability. The annual Open Source Programme Report — publishing contribution statistics, compliance rates, community health indicators, and strategic alignment — is the instrument through which the OSPO demonstrates its institutional value and accounts for the resources it consumes.
